Why Awendaw Is Charleston's Best-Kept Secret for Homebuyers

Austin Garland, Realtor
The Cassina Group · Mount Pleasant, SC
March 2, 2026

If you ask most people about buying a home near Charleston, they'll mention Mount Pleasant, Isle of Palms, maybe Sullivan's Island or James Island. Almost nobody mentions Awendaw — and that's precisely why it deserves attention.

Awendaw is a small, unincorporated community sitting about 20 miles north of Mount Pleasant along Highway 17. It borders the Francis Marion National Forest to the west and the Cape Romain National Wildlife Refuge to the east, putting it at the intersection of two of the most ecologically significant landscapes on the East Coast. For homebuyers willing to trade a longer commute for more land, more privacy, and significantly more accessible pricing, Awendaw is one of the most compelling options in the greater Charleston market right now.

Where exactly is Awendaw and how far is it from Charleston?

Awendaw sits along Highway 17 (also called the Coastal Highway) between Mount Pleasant and McClellanville. The center of the community is roughly 30 minutes north of the Ravenel Bridge and about 20 minutes north of the main commercial areas of Mount Pleasant.

During off-peak hours, the drive to downtown Charleston is straightforward — Highway 17 South to the Ravenel Bridge. During morning rush hour, the commute can stretch to 40–50 minutes depending on traffic through Mount Pleasant. This is the primary trade-off with Awendaw, and it's worth making the drive at 7:30 AM on a weekday before you commit to buying here.

That said, the remote work shift has significantly changed the calculus for many buyers. If you work from home even two or three days a week, the commute math becomes much more favorable, and you gain access to a lifestyle that's nearly impossible to find closer to the city.

What kind of properties are available in Awendaw?

Awendaw's real estate character is fundamentally different from Mount Pleasant or Isle of Palms. You won't find master-planned subdivisions with pools and tennis courts. What you will find is space.

Properties in Awendaw typically feature larger lots — anywhere from one to ten+ acres — with a mix of home styles: traditional Lowcountry homes, custom-built properties, renovated farmhouses, and newer construction on cleared lots. Some properties back directly to the Francis Marion National Forest, offering essentially infinite backyard space with no development risk on the neighboring land.

Waterfront options exist as well, particularly along the creeks and tributaries that feed into Bulls Bay and the Intracoastal Waterway. Deep-water access is possible in some locations, though you'll want to verify dock permitting carefully — the proximity to wildlife refuges means permitting can be more complex than in established marinas.

Price points in Awendaw are notably more accessible. Where a single-family home in central Mount Pleasant might start in the mid-$400Ks for modest square footage on a quarter-acre lot, comparable or larger homes in Awendaw can be found in the $300K–$600K range with significantly more land. Higher-end waterfront or custom properties exist in the $700K–$1.2M range, but even the premium end of Awendaw undercuts comparable Mount Pleasant properties substantially.

For the price of a townhome in Park West, you can often get a three-bedroom house on two acres in Awendaw with forest or marsh views.

What is there to do in Awendaw?

If your definition of "things to do" centers on restaurants, shopping centers, and nightlife, Awendaw will feel quiet. If your definition centers on outdoor recreation and access to nature, Awendaw is one of the best-positioned communities in the entire state.

The Francis Marion National Forest covers over 260,000 acres and sits directly adjacent to Awendaw. It offers hiking, mountain biking, horseback riding, hunting, fishing, and camping. The Swamp Fox Passage of the Palmetto Trail runs through it — a 47-mile hiking and biking trail that passes through diverse ecosystems from longleaf pine forests to cypress swamps.

To the east, the Cape Romain National Wildlife Refuge protects 66,000 acres of barrier islands, salt marshes, and coastal waterways. Bulls Island, accessible by a short ferry from the Garris Landing boat ramp in Awendaw, is one of the most pristine barrier islands remaining on the East Coast — 5,000 acres of undeveloped maritime forest, beaches, and wetlands. It's the kind of place that feels like you've stepped back 200 years.

Sewee Outpost, a local favorite, serves as an informal community hub — a combination restaurant, farm stand, and oyster roast venue that draws people from across the Charleston metro. The Center for Birds of Prey in Awendaw is a nationally recognized raptor rehabilitation facility that offers public tours and educational programs.

For daily needs, Awendaw residents typically drive 15–20 minutes south to Mount Pleasant's shopping corridors for groceries, medical care, and retail. This is part of the rural trade-off, but most people who choose Awendaw aren't looking for urban convenience — they're looking for a different way of living.

Is Awendaw a good investment right now?

From a pure market dynamics perspective, Awendaw has several factors working in its favor. Mount Pleasant's northward growth continues to push development (and prices) closer to Awendaw's borders. Highway 17 improvements have shortened effective commute times. And the broader trend of remote work has expanded the buyer pool for communities that were previously considered "too far" from employment centers.

The conservation land that borders Awendaw — the national forest and wildlife refuge — effectively guarantees that the natural character of the area won't change. Your neighbor to the west will always be 260,000 acres of protected forest, not a new subdivision. That kind of guaranteed preservation is increasingly rare and increasingly valued by buyers seeking long-term quality of life.

The risk factor to consider is liquidity. Awendaw properties typically take longer to sell than comparable Mount Pleasant homes because the buyer pool is smaller and more specific. If you need to sell quickly, that's worth factoring into your decision. This is a market for people who are buying a lifestyle, not flipping for quick returns.

What should I look for when buying in Awendaw?

Several Awendaw-specific considerations that I walk my buyers through:

Well and septic systems are common in Awendaw since much of the area isn't served by municipal water and sewer. If you're coming from a city environment, understand that well water quality varies and septic systems require regular maintenance. Always get a well water test and septic inspection during due diligence.

Flood zones apply in Awendaw as well, particularly for properties near creeks, marshes, and the Intracoastal. The same flood insurance considerations that apply on Isle of Palms apply here, though premiums tend to be lower given the lower property values.

Road access — some Awendaw properties are accessed by unpaved roads or private easements. Verify the road maintenance responsibility and legal access before closing. This is especially important for properties deeper into the forest areas.

Wildlife considerations are real. Deer, wild boar, alligators, and various snakes are part of the landscape. This is rural Lowcountry living, and for most people who choose Awendaw, the wildlife is a feature, not a bug. But if that's not your thing, be honest with yourself about it.
